History of Orthodoxy

From the descent of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost to the present day — the unbroken story of the faith once delivered to the saints.

Orthodox Christianity does not understand itself as one tradition among many that emerged after the New Testament, but as the continuous and unbroken life of the Body of Christ — born at Pentecost, sustained by the Holy Spirit, and present in every Divine Liturgy celebrated in His name. This timeline traces the key events, councils, and turning points through which that faith has been preserved, defended, tested, and handed on across twenty centuries. Select any event to read more.

❦
Apostolic Era
c. 33–100
c. 33 Pentecost — The Church Is Born ►

Fifty days after the Resurrection, the Holy Spirit descended upon the Apostles gathered in Jerusalem. With the sound of a mighty wind and tongues of fire, each was filled with the Spirit and began to speak in other tongues. Three thousand were baptized that day. For the Orthodox Church, Pentecost is the full revelation of the Holy Trinity to the world and the beginning of the Church’s life in the Spirit.

The Church that began at Pentecost is the same Church that exists today — guided by the same Spirit, celebrating the same Eucharist, in unbroken continuity with the Apostles.

Key Themes
Founding of the Church Holy Spirit Acts 2
33–100 The Apostolic Mission ►

Guided by the Holy Spirit, the Apostles carried the risen Christ to the ends of the known world. St. Peter and St. Paul were martyred in Rome. St. John, the last of the Twelve, died in Ephesus around 100. St. Andrew evangelized the regions that would become Greece and Byzantium. St. Thomas reached India. St. Mark founded the Church of Alexandria. The structures of the Church — bishop, presbyter, deacon — were established in this period, as were the earliest liturgical patterns and the sacraments.

The letters of St. Ignatius of Antioch (martyred c. 107) reveal a fully episcopal church already in place within a generation of the Apostles — the bishop as the center of the Eucharistic community, the Eucharist as the true Body and Blood of Christ. The faith was not gradually developed; it was received, lived, and handed on. The same chain of succession — bishop to bishop, generation to generation — runs unbroken to the present.

Key Themes
Apostolic Succession Church Structure Worldwide Mission Eucharist
c. 50 Council of Jerusalem ►

When a dispute arose over the requirements for Gentile believers, the Apostles and elders gathered in Jerusalem to discern together how the nations would enter the life of Christ. Their decision carries the defining phrase of all Christian councils: “It seemed good to the Holy Spirit and to us” (Acts 15:28). The Church does not legislate from human authority alone — she gathers in prayer and listens to the Spirit who guides her into all truth.

This first council establishes the conciliar pattern that the Church carries through every age. The Apostles did not rule by individual authority. They assembled, deliberated, and spoke with one voice. Every Ecumenical Council that follows — from Nicaea to Nicaea II — inherits this authority: the Church gathered, guided by the Holy Spirit, proclaiming not new teaching but the faith once delivered to the saints.

Key Themes
Conciliarity Holy Spirit Acts 15 Gentile Mission
Age of Martyrs
64–313
64–313 The Roman Persecutions ►

For nearly 250 years the Church grew under intermittent and sometimes severe persecution. Beginning under Nero (64 AD) and reaching its most intense phase under Diocletian (303–311), Christians faced execution, imprisonment, and loss of property for refusing to offer sacrifice to the imperial gods.

Rather than destroying the Church, the blood of the martyrs became its seed. The courage of those who died — from the soldier St. George to Perpetua and Felicity — drew converts and demonstrated the transforming power of the resurrection faith. The Church developed its theology of martyrdom and veneration of the saints during this period.

Key Themes
Martyrdom Veneration of Saints Tertullian: blood of martyrs is seed
313 Edict of Milan — Christianity Legalized ►

The Emperors Constantine I and Licinius granted religious toleration throughout the Empire and restored confiscated Church property. Constantine himself had converted after a vision of the Cross before the Battle of Milvian Bridge (312) and became the first Christian emperor. Persecution ended; basilicas were built; the episcopate gained public recognition.

The Church’s relationship with the state — neither fusion nor separation, but a complex cooperation called symphonia — begins here. The Orthodox Church venerates Constantine and his mother Helena as Equal-to-the-Apostles.

Key Themes
St. Constantine Equal-to-Apostles Church & State Symphonia
Age of the Seven Councils
325–787
325 First Ecumenical Council — Nicaea I ►

Approximately 318 bishops gathered to address the Arian heresy, which taught that the Son of God was the greatest of creatures but not truly God. The Council defined the Son as homoousios — of one essence with the Father — and produced the first form of the Nicene Creed. St. Athanasius of Alexandria, then a young deacon, was the decisive voice. The date of Pascha was standardized.

The decades after Nicaea saw intense struggle — Arianism was favored by successive emperors — but Athanasius held the orthodox faith alone against the world (Athanasius contra mundum). Nicaea’s theology was vindicated at Constantinople in 381.

Key Themes
Against Arianism Homoousios Nicene Creed St. Athanasius
381 Second Ecumenical Council — Constantinople I ►

Convened by Emperor Theodosius I, the Second Council completed the Nicene Creed by adding the full teaching on the Holy Spirit, responding to the Pneumatomachians who denied the Spirit’s divinity. The Cappadocian Fathers — Basil the Great, Gregory the Theologian, and Gregory of Nyssa — had laid the theological groundwork. Apollinarianism (which denied Christ a full human soul) was also condemned.

The Creed produced here — the Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed — is the Symbol of Faith recited at every Divine Liturgy. It has never been altered by the Orthodox Church.

Key Themes
Holy Spirit Cappadocian Fathers Symbol of Faith Completed
431 Third Ecumenical Council — Ephesus ►

Convened to address Nestorianism, which divided Christ into two persons and therefore refused to call Mary the Theotokos. St. Cyril of Alexandria led the orthodox defense. The Council affirmed that Mary is truly the Mother of God: because the one she bore is a single divine person, she is rightly called Theotokos — God-bearer.

The Nestorian church survived in Persia and eventually reached India and China. The Council’s definition remains foundational to Orthodox Mariology and Christology.

Key Themes
Theotokos Against Nestorianism St. Cyril of Alexandria
451 Fourth Ecumenical Council — Chalcedon ►

The largest of the early councils (over 500 bishops), convened against Eutychianism (Monophysitism), which collapsed the two natures of Christ into one. The Definition of Chalcedon proclaimed Christ as one person in two natures — “without confusion, without change, without division, without separation.” These four adverbs are among the most precise formulations in Christian theological history.

This is not philosophical precision for its own sake. If Christ’s humanity is absorbed into His divinity, our own humanity cannot truly be assumed, healed, and deified. Chalcedon defends the very ground of our salvation: God truly became one of us so that we might truly become partakers of the divine nature (2 Pet. 1:4). Chalcedon caused a lasting schism with the Oriental Orthodox churches (Coptic, Ethiopian, Armenian, Syriac), though modern theological dialogue has clarified much of the original disagreement.

Key Themes
Two Natures of Christ Against Monophysitism Theosis Oriental Orthodox Schism
553 Fifth Ecumenical Council — Constantinople II ►

Convened under Emperor Justinian, the Fifth Council addressed lingering Nestorian tendencies in certain theological writings known as the “Three Chapters.” The council reaffirmed that it is always the one eternal Person of the Son who acts — in His divinity and in His humanity. There is no separation of subjects in Christ: the same Son who was born of the Virgin is the same Son who rose from the dead.

The council also sought, with pastoral care, to draw those estranged by Chalcedon back into communion — a witness to the Church’s persistent desire not for theological victory but for unity in the fullness of truth. The Holy Spirit who guides the Church into truth also moves her toward the healing of division. This pastoral concern is itself a mark of the Orthodox phronema.

Key Themes
Against Nestorianism Unity of Christ’s Person Pastoral Unity
680–681 Sixth Ecumenical Council — Constantinople III ►

Monothelitism taught that Christ possessed only one will, His human will swallowed up by the divine. Constantinople III proclaimed the truth: Christ has two wills — divine and human — perfectly united, His human will freely surrendered to the Father in love: “Not My will, but Yours, be done” (Lk. 22:42). This is not erasure of the human will but its perfect healing and offering.

This truth has direct bearing on our salvation. Our human wills are disordered by sin and need not destruction but restoration. Christ’s human will — perfectly offered to the Father — is both the model and the means of our own theosis. St. Maximus the Confessor, who suffered exile, imprisonment, and the cutting out of his tongue and right hand for defending this truth, is one of the great confessors of the Orthodox faith. He is venerated on January 21.

Key Themes
Two Wills of Christ Against Monothelitism St. Maximus the Confessor Theosis
692 Council in Trullo — The Quinisext Council ►

The Council in Trullo (so named for the domed hall of the imperial palace) was convened to supply the disciplinary canons that the Fifth and Sixth Ecumenical Councils had left unfinished. Its 102 canons codify the canonical discipline of the Orthodox Church: the liturgical life, iconographic norms, fasting practice, clerical marriage, and the boundaries of episcopal authority. It is recognized by the Orthodox Church as having full canonical authority — effectively the disciplinary completion of Councils V and VI.

Rome refused to ratify the Council in Trullo, rejecting several canons that contradicted emerging Western practice — particularly those permitting married clergy and mandating the depiction of Christ in human form rather than as a lamb. This refusal is itself an early marker of the liturgical and disciplinary divergence that would deepen over the following centuries. The canons of Trullo remain binding for the Orthodox Church to this day.

Key Themes
Canon Law Church Discipline East-West Divergence Liturgical Norms
787 Seventh Ecumenical Council — Nicaea II ►

The last of the Seven Ecumenical Councils, convened to end the first period of Iconoclasm (726–787), during which imperial authorities ordered the destruction of icons and persecuted those who venerated them. The Council affirmed the veneration of icons on the basis of the Incarnation: because the Son of God took visible human flesh, He can be depicted, and to honor the image is to honor the person.

A second Iconoclasm (814–842) ended with the Triumph of Orthodoxy in 843, still celebrated on the first Sunday of Great Lent.

Key Themes
Veneration of Icons Against Iconoclasm Triumph of Orthodoxy
Post-Conciliar Byzantine Era
843–988
843 The Triumph of Orthodoxy ►

On the first Sunday of Great Lent, 843, icons were restored to the churches of Constantinople, ending the second period of Iconoclasm. The Empress Theodora, acting as regent, convened a council that vindicated the theology of Nicaea II (787). Patriarch Methodius led a solemn procession through the city with the holy icons. This Sunday has been celebrated ever since as the Triumph of Orthodoxy — the first Sunday of Great Lent in the Orthodox Church throughout the world.

The Triumph is not merely the restoration of religious art. It is the victory of the Incarnation itself — the Church’s proclamation that God truly took on flesh and can be depicted in matter, that the material world is capable of bearing the divine, and that the truth the Holy Spirit has given the Church cannot be extinguished by imperial decree. At the Triumph of Orthodoxy service, the Church still sings: “This is the faith of the Apostles; this is the faith of the Fathers; this is the faith of the Orthodox; this faith has established the universe.”

Key Themes
Veneration of Icons Incarnation Against Iconoclasm Great Lent St. Theodora the Empress
c. 863 Mission of Sts. Cyril and Methodius ►

The brothers Cyril and Methodius, scholars from Thessaloniki, were sent by the Patriarch of Constantinople on a missionary journey to the Slavic peoples of Moravia. Rather than imposing the faith in Greek or Latin, they translated the Scriptures and the Divine Liturgy into the Slavic language — and created an alphabet (Glagolitic, the precursor to Cyrillic) to do so. The gospel was incarnated into a new tongue, and the Slavic world was opened to the Orthodox faith.

The missionary theology of Cyril and Methodius is the theology of Pentecost: the Holy Spirit speaks to every nation in its own language (Acts 2:6). Orthodoxy is not a Greek religion or a Byzantine culture — it is the faith of the universal Church, offered to every people in their own tongue. Their mission planted the seeds from which Russian, Serbian, Bulgarian, and other Slavic Orthodoxies would grow. They are venerated as Equal-to-the-Apostles, and their feast is celebrated on May 11. Cyril fell asleep in the Lord in Rome in 869; Methodius continued the work among the Slavs until his death in 885.

Key Themes
Slavic Mission Equal-to-the-Apostles Liturgical Translation Pentecost Theology
988 Baptism of Rus’ ►

Prince Vladimir of Kiev, having sent emissaries to investigate the religions of the known world, was drawn by the report of his envoys who had witnessed the Divine Liturgy at Hagia Sophia: “We did not know whether we were in heaven or on earth” (Primary Chronicle). Vladimir was baptized, married the Byzantine princess Anna, and ordered the Christianization of Kievan Rus’. The Dnieper River became a site of mass baptism. The Church of the Rus’ was born.

The Baptism of Rus’ in 988 is one of the most consequential events in the history of the Orthodox Church. It planted the faith in the soil from which Russian, Ukrainian, and Belarusian Orthodoxy would grow — and through Russia, would spread across Siberia, Alaska, Japan, and China. The Russian Orthodox Church today is the largest Orthodox church in the world. St. Vladimir is venerated as Equal-to-the-Apostles, and his feast is celebrated on July 15. His grandmother, St. Olga, who had herself been baptized in Constantinople, is celebrated on July 11.

Key Themes
Evangelization of the Slavs St. Vladimir Equal-to-the-Apostles Divine Liturgy Russian Orthodoxy
Schism & the Medieval Church
867–1453
867 The Photian Controversy — Filioque Challenge ►

Patriarch Photios of Constantinople issued the first definitive theological critique of the Western church’s addition of the Filioque to the Nicene Creed. In his Mystagogy of the Holy Spirit, Photios argued that the procession of the Spirit from both Father and Son undermined the monarchy of the Father and introduced a fundamental error into Trinitarian theology.

Pope Nicholas I refused to recognize Photios’ election and the controversy became the first major open rupture between Rome and Constantinople. It was healed, but the Filioque remained embedded in Western practice. The Orthodox Church venerates Photios as a saint and Father of the Church.

Key Themes
Filioque St. Photios the Great East-West Tension
879–880 Council of Constantinople — Restoration of St. Photios ►

A council of over 380 bishops assembled in Constantinople under the presidency of Patriarch Photios, with papal legates of Pope John VIII in attendance. Photios was formally vindicated and restored. More significantly, the council issued a canon explicitly forbidding any addition or alteration to the Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed — condemning the Filioque by name as an unauthorized innovation. Pope John VIII initially accepted the council’s decisions. Many Orthodox theologians recognize this council as the Eighth Ecumenical Council.

The council’s condemnation of the Filioque is the Church’s definitive theological and canonical response to the Western addition. It establishes three truths simultaneously: the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father alone, as taught in the Creed of the Ecumenical Councils; no bishop — including the Bishop of Rome — holds authority to alter what the councils have defined; and the conciliar mind of the Church, not any individual hierarch, is the guardian of the Apostolic faith.

Key Themes
Filioque Condemned Conciliar Authority St. Photios the Great Creedal Integrity
1054 The Great Schism ►

On July 16, 1054, Cardinal Humbert of Rome placed a bull of excommunication on the altar of Hagia Sophia directed at Patriarch Michael Cerularius of Constantinople, who excommunicated the Roman legates in return. The formal break between the Eastern and Western churches — long building over centuries — is dated to this moment.

The underlying causes ran deep: the Filioque addition to the Creed, growing claims of papal universal jurisdiction, differences in liturgical practice, and disputes over newly evangelized territories. The mutual excommunications of 1054 were lifted in 1964, though full communion has not been restored.

Key Themes
East-West Division Papal Claims Filioque

1204 Sack of Constantinople — Fourth Crusade ►

The armies of the Fourth Crusade, diverted by Venetian interests and Byzantine politics, sacked Constantinople. Hagia Sophia was looted; relics, manuscripts, and sacred art were destroyed or carried west; a Latin emperor was enthroned and a Latin Patriarch installed. Pope Innocent III condemned the action, but the damage was permanent.

For Orthodox Christians, 1204 remains a wound of incomprehensible betrayal — Christian armies desecrating the greatest Christian city on earth. The Byzantine Empire recovered Constantinople in 1261 but never regained its former strength.

Key Themes
Crusades Latin Empire Hagia Sophia
1341–1351 The Palamite Councils — Uncreated Energies Defined ►

The hesychast controversy erupted when Barlaam of Calabria attacked the monks of Athos who claimed to behold the uncreated divine light in prayer. St. Gregory Palamas defended the monks by articulating the distinction between God’s unknowable essence and His uncreated energies, which are truly God and truly participable by creatures.

The Councils of Constantinople (1341, 1347, 1351) vindicated Palamas and condemned Barlaam. The doctrine of the divine energies is now recognized as a dogma of the Orthodox Church, with profound implications for theosis and the spiritual life. The second Sunday of Great Lent is dedicated to St. Gregory Palamas.

Key Themes
Uncreated Energies St. Gregory Palamas Theosis Hesychasm
Ottoman Period
1453–1821
1453 Fall of Constantinople ►

On May 29, 1453, Ottoman forces under Sultan Mehmed II breached the walls after a 53-day siege. The last Byzantine Emperor, Constantine XI Palaiologos, died fighting. Hagia Sophia, built by Justinian in 537, was converted to a mosque the same day. The end of a thousand-year empire and the loss of the political protector of Orthodoxy.

But the Church survived. The Ecumenical Patriarchate was granted authority over all Orthodox Christians in the Ottoman Empire. Scholars fled to the West carrying manuscripts that helped ignite the Renaissance. The Orthodox faith endured under centuries of subjugation without losing its integrity.

Key Themes
End of Byzantium Hagia Sophia Ecumenical Patriarchate
1453–1821 The Church Under Ottoman Rule ►

For nearly four centuries, the Ecumenical Patriarchate (the Phanar) served as both ecclesiastical head and civil protector of all Orthodox Christians under Ottoman rule. The period produced its own saints: the Neomartyrs who chose death over conversion to Islam, including St. Kosmas Aitolos, who evangelized Greek and Albanian populations.

Mount Athos remained a living center of Orthodox spiritual life and scholarship throughout. The Philokalia was compiled during this period (1782) by Nikodemos the Hagiorite — a testimony to the enduring vitality of the hesychast tradition under subjugation.

Key Themes
Neomartyrs Phanar Mount Athos Philokalia
Modern Era
1821–Present
1821–1900s National Churches & New Autocephalies ►

The Greek War of Independence (1821–1829) began a new era: the emergence of autocephalous national churches as Balkan and Slavic peoples won independence from Ottoman rule. Greece, Serbia, Romania, Bulgaria, and others established independent Orthodox churches through a complex process involving the Ecumenical Patriarchate.

This period also saw the great missionary expansion of Russian Orthodoxy into Alaska (St. Herman, St. Innocent), Japan (St. Nicholas of Japan), China, and Korea. The Orthodox Church in America traces its roots to the Russian mission that arrived in Alaska in 1794.

Key Themes
Autocephaly National Churches St. Herman of Alaska Orthodox Mission
1917–1991 The Russian Church Under Communism ►

The Bolshevik Revolution unleashed the most severe persecution of Christians in history. Tens of thousands of clergy, monastics, and faithful were executed or sent to labor camps under Lenin and Stalin. By 1939, fewer than 500 of Russia’s 54,000 pre-revolutionary churches remained open. The New Martyrs and Confessors of Russia — canonized in 2000 — number in the tens of thousands.

Among the most venerated are the Royal Martyrs (Tsar Nicholas II and his family), Patriarch Tikhon, and Metropolitan Benjamin of Petrograd. With the fall of the Soviet Union in 1991, the Church began a dramatic restoration. Russian Orthodoxy today is the largest Orthodox church in the world by baptized membership.

Key Themes
New Martyrs of Russia Royal Martyrs Soviet Persecution
20th–21st Century Orthodoxy in the West & the Global Diaspora ►

Immigration waves from Greece, Eastern Europe, the Middle East, and Russia brought Orthodoxy to Western Europe, North America, Australia, and beyond. The 20th century also saw significant conversions to Orthodoxy in the West, including entire communities from Evangelical and Anglican backgrounds. Orthodoxy is no longer simply an “immigrant church” but a living tradition taking root in new soils.

The Holy and Great Council convened in Crete in 2016 was the first pan-Orthodox council in over a millennium, though several major churches declined to attend, leaving its reception complex. The question of how the autocephalous churches relate — and the role of the Ecumenical Patriarchate as first among equals — remains a central ecclesiological question of our time.

Key Themes
Western Orthodoxy Great Council of Crete 2016 Primacy & Conciliarity Diaspora
