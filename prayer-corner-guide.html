Setting Up a Prayer Corner

A complete guide to the Orthodox home prayer corner — its theology, its purpose, how to set it up, how to use it, and how it connects your home to the life of the Church.

← Back to Prayer Corner

What Is a Prayer Corner?

In the Orthodox home, one corner of a room — traditionally the eastern corner — is set apart as a sacred space. It is not a decoration or a museum piece. It is the place where the family or the individual gathers before God morning and evening, where candles are lit and prayers are offered, and where the home itself participates in the prayer of the Church.

The practice of facing east in prayer is among the most ancient Christian customs, defended by St. Basil the Great as an apostolic tradition: we face east because we are facing the rising Sun of Righteousness — Christ Himself — and because the Second Coming will come from the east, as Our Lord promised: "For as the lightning comes from the east and shines as far as the west, so will be the coming of the Son of Man" (Matthew 24:27). The prayer corner, oriented toward the east, places the Christian physically in that posture of expectation.

The prayer corner is not private devotion happening alongside Church life — it is the Church's life extended into the home. It is the place where morning and evening the Christian returns to God, not from some private spiritual preference, but as a member of the Body of Christ continuing the unceasing prayer of the whole Church.

St. Basil the Great — On the Holy Spirit, Ch. 27 "We all look to the East in our prayers, but few of us know that we are seeking our own ancient homeland, the Paradise which God planted in Eden, in the East."

The Home as Domestic Church

From the very beginning, the Church has understood the Christian home as a place of worship. The earliest Christians broke bread and prayed together in private houses before any church buildings existed: "And day by day, attending the temple together and breaking bread in their homes, they received their food with glad and generous hearts, praising God" (Acts 2:46). St. Paul greeted "the church in your house" — the church, not merely the family (Romans 16:5; 1 Corinthians 16:19).

The Orthodox tradition speaks of the Christian home as the domestic church — not as a metaphor but as a theological reality. The home is not a secular space with a religious corner attached. When it is ordered around prayer, Scripture, the keeping of fasts, the celebration of feasts, and the love of God and neighbor, the home itself becomes a place of the Church's presence in the world. The prayer corner is the visible center of that reality.

This means that the prayer corner is not primarily about personal piety or private religious feeling. It is about the family or the individual Christian standing before God as a member of the Body of Christ — continuing, at home, the prayer that was offered at the Divine Liturgy and that will be offered again. Home prayer flows from the Liturgy and returns to it. The prayer corner is the bridge between the altar and the kitchen table.

St. John of Kronstadt — My Life in Christ "Your home is your church. Consecrate it to God by prayer and holy life, and let the lamp burning before the holy icons be the sign that Christ is the Light of your household."

Getting Started — Step by Step

Begin simply. The prayer corner does not need to be elaborate or expensive. What matters is that it exists, that it is used, and that it grows over time as your prayer life grows.

1
Choose a corner or wall
Ideally the eastern wall or corner of a room — a bedroom, a quiet living space, or wherever you will return to consistently. If facing east is not possible in your home, any quiet, dedicated space will do. The key is consistency: this should be a place you return to every day.
2
Place a shelf or small table
A simple wooden shelf or small table holds your icons at eye level or slightly above. The icons should face you as you pray. They need not be expensive or numerous — one or two icons are sufficient to begin.
3
Begin with the essential icons
Start simply. An icon of Christ (the Pantocrator) and an icon of the Theotokos are the foundation. These two are traditionally placed together — Christ on the right, the Theotokos on the left, as they appear on the iconostasis of every Orthodox church.
4
Add an oil lamp or candle
A vigil lamp (kandili) burning before the icons is the ancient mark of Orthodox home prayer. The light speaks of Christ who is the Light of the world, and of our prayers ascending before Him. Some families keep it burning continuously; others light it only during prayer. Either is appropriate.
5
Add incense (optional)
Incense has been part of Christian worship since the Old Testament. The Psalmist prays: "Let my prayer arise as incense before Thee, the lifting up of my hands as an evening sacrifice" (Psalm 141:2 LXX). A simple incense holder with frankincense or liturgical incense is appropriate. It is optional, but it brings the senses into prayer in the tradition of the Church.
6
Keep a prayer book nearby
An Orthodox prayer book should be within reach. The Jordanville Prayer Book and the Ancient Faith Prayer Book are both widely used in English. The prayers in these books are not suggestions — they are the received form of Orthodox morning and evening prayer, handed down in the tradition.
7
Add a prayer rope
A prayer rope (komboskini or chotki) is used to count repetitions of the Jesus Prayer. Keep one near your icons or carry it with you. The prayer rope is not only for monastics — it is a practical instrument of prayer for any faithful Christian.
There is no single required arrangement. The steps above are a guide, not a law. Speak with your priest about what is appropriate for your home and circumstances. What matters is not the beauty or completeness of the corner, but the sincerity and regularity of the prayer offered before it.
Family Prayer The prayer corner is not only for individuals. In the Orthodox home, the family prays together — parents and children standing before the icons, morning and evening. Even young children can participate: they stand, make the Sign of the Cross, and hear the prayers said aloud. This shared prayer before the icons is one of the most formative gifts a Christian parent can give a child. Speak with your priest about how to adapt the rule for the rhythms of your household.

Which Icons to Have

Icons are not decorations, and they are not physical presences of the saints. The Seventh Ecumenical Council (Nicaea II, 787) teaches that the honor given to the icon passes to the prototype — the person depicted. The icon is a window into the Kingdom of God: a sacred image through which we venerate the one depicted, asking for their intercession, and through which the person depicted is connected to our prayer. We bow before an icon as we would bow before the saint in person — not worshipping the image, but venerating the one it represents.

Begin with one or two icons and add others as you grow. Do not try to acquire every icon at once. An icon should be chosen with care, acquired at the right time, and blessed by a priest before being placed in the prayer corner.

Christ the Pantocrator
The foundational icon of any prayer corner. Christ as Lord of all, blessing and holding the Gospels. Always placed on the right.
The Theotokos
The Mother of God, the great intercessor. Often depicted as the Hodigitria (She who shows the Way) or the Eleousa (Tenderness). Placed to the left of Christ.
Your patron saint
The saint whose name you bear, or whose feast falls on your birthday. Your heavenly companion, model, and intercessor before God.
The feast of your parish
The feast to which your parish church is dedicated — a visible connection between your home prayer and the life of your parish community.
The Resurrection (Anastasis)
The icon of Pascha — Christ descending into Hades and raising Adam and Eve. The central mystery of the faith, present in every Orthodox home.
The Holy Trinity (Rublev)
Andrei Rublev's icon of the three angels at Abraham's table — the most beloved icon of the Holy Trinity in the Orthodox world.

How to Pray at the Prayer Corner

For those new to Orthodoxy, the physical posture and practice of prayer before the icons may be unfamiliar. Here is what the tradition teaches.

■
Stand
The normal posture for Orthodox prayer is standing — upright before God, as the redeemed stand in His presence. Standing signifies the Resurrection: we pray as those who have been raised from death with Christ. Kneeling and prostrations are appropriate at other times, but standing is the primary posture. If health makes standing difficult for an extended time, adapt as needed.
■
Begin with the Sign of the Cross
Before beginning any prayer, make the Sign of the Cross slowly and deliberately — touching forehead, breast, right shoulder, left shoulder, and joining the hands. This is not a formality. It is a complete act of faith: the Trinity confessed in the three joined fingers, the two natures of Christ in the two fingers against the palm, and the Cross of Christ traced upon the body. Begin and end every prayer session with it.
■
Make small bows and prostrations
A small bow from the waist (metania) accompanies many of the prayers — typically at "Glory to the Father," at "Lord have mercy," and after receiving a blessing. Full prostrations (touching the floor with both knees and forehead) are made especially during Great Lent. Prostrations are not made on Sundays, from Pascha through Pentecost, or on the Great Feasts — the Church stands in joy on these days. Your priest can instruct you further.
■
Venerate the icons
At the beginning and end of prayer, approach the icons, make the Sign of the Cross, and bow. You may kiss the icon — typically the hand of Christ, the Gospel book He holds, or the border of the icon. You do not kiss the face. This veneration is an act of love and connection with the saint depicted, not worship of the image itself.
■
Pray with attention
The most important element of prayer is not its length or its form, but attention. St. Theophan the Recluse taught: "The principal thing is to stand before God with the mind in the heart, and to go on standing before Him unceasingly day and night, until the end of life." If your mind wanders during prayer — and it will — gently return it without distress. The act of returning is itself an act of prayer.

The Daily Prayer Rule

The Orthodox tradition gives us a morning and evening prayer rule — a set of prayers to be said daily before the icons. The morning rule is an offering: you give the coming day to God before it begins. The evening rule is an examination and an entrustment: you review the day before God and place the night in His hands.

AM
Morning Prayers
Light the lamp, make the Sign of the Cross, and begin. Start with the Trisagion prayers, then the morning prayers from the prayer book. Read them attentively — not quickly. These typically take 10–20 minutes when read with care. The purpose is not to finish the prayers, but to stand before God and offer the day.
PM
Evening Prayers
The evening rule mirrors the morning — Trisagion, evening prayers, and a brief examination of conscience: what was offered to God today, what was wasted, what was done in sin. End with the Jesus Prayer on the prayer rope. Place yourself in God's hands before sleep.
St. Theophan the Recluse "The rule exists to serve prayer, not prayer to serve the rule. If you are rushed, say fewer prayers with full attention rather than many prayers hastily."

How to Begin — Your First Week

The most common mistake when beginning a prayer corner is trying to do everything at once. A full morning and evening rule, candles, incense, prostrations — all of it immediately — is too much for a beginner to sustain. Consistency is more important than completeness. Begin with very little and add gradually.

Days 1–2
Stand before your icons
Make the Sign of the Cross
Pray the Lord's Prayer once
Make the Sign of the Cross to close
Days 3–4
Add the Trisagion Prayers before the Lord's Prayer
Light the lamp while you pray
Read Psalm 50 aloud
Days 5–7
Add one morning prayer from the prayer book
Say the Jesus Prayer 10 times on the prayer rope in the evening
End with a brief word to God in your own words
After the First Week
Speak with your priest
Establish the rule together
Grow one prayer at a time
Do not rush
The goal of the first week is simply to begin. A prayer corner that is used imperfectly every day is worth infinitely more than one that is planned perfectly and used rarely.

When You Struggle

Every person who prays will encounter difficulty. This is not a sign that something is wrong. It is the normal condition of the Christian prayer life, and the tradition prepares us for it honestly.

■
When your mind wanders constantly
Distraction during prayer is the ordinary experience of every Christian, from beginners to those who have prayed for decades. The act of noticing that your mind has wandered and returning it gently to God — without frustration, without self-condemnation — is itself an act of prayer. Do not fight distraction violently; simply return. The Fathers are unanimous on this.
■
When prayer feels dry and lifeless
Dryness — the absence of feeling, warmth, or consolation in prayer — is not the absence of God. It is often a purification, a time in which the soul is being weaned from its dependence on spiritual feelings and taught to seek God for His own sake. Continue the rule faithfully during dryness. Prayer offered without feeling, simply because God deserves it, is among the purest forms of prayer.
■
When life is too busy or chaotic
Do not abandon the rule when life becomes difficult — reduce it. Say one prayer instead of ten. Stand before the icons for two minutes instead of twenty. Light the lamp and make the Sign of the Cross. Even the smallest act of turning toward God in a moment of chaos is an act of faithfulness. The rule contracts and expands with life's seasons — it does not disappear.
■
When you have missed days or weeks
Simply return. The prayer corner is waiting. God is not keeping a ledger of missed prayers. Rise and pray, as the prodigal son rose and returned to his father without waiting until he had earned the right to return. The greatest mistake is not missing the rule — it is deciding that because you have missed it, you cannot return to it today.
St. John Chrysostom "Though you may have missed prayer for many days, do not let shame keep you from returning. God does not count how long you were absent — He rejoices that you have come back."

A Word on Simplicity

The prayer corner can become its own spiritual trap if it is treated as a project rather than a place of prayer. There is a very human tendency — especially when beginning a new practice — to focus on the appearance and completeness of the corner rather than on the prayer offered before it. This is a mistake the tradition warns against directly.

A single blessed icon, a candle, and a sincere heart are more than sufficient for a lifetime of prayer. The most spiritually formed Christians in the Orthodox world have often prayed before the simplest of corners. The beauty of the corner should grow over time as a natural expression of love — not as the precondition for prayer, and not as a substitute for it.

Do not delay beginning because the corner is not ready. Do not spend more time arranging it than praying before it. Do not measure your prayer life by the number of icons or the quality of your oil lamp. The prayer corner exists to serve prayer. If it begins to serve aesthetics, it has lost its purpose.

St. Isaac the Syrian "A man who knows his own weakness has attained something greater than one who sees visions of angels. Begin from where you are, with what you have, and God will lead you further."

How It Changes Through the Year

The prayer corner is not static — it breathes with the liturgical year. This is one of the ways the home is a domestic church: it reflects, at a smaller scale, the same rhythm of fasting and feasting, mourning and joy, that the Church lives through the year. The corner's atmosphere changes as the seasons change, and the prayer rule adjusts with it.

■
Great Lent
Add the Prayer of St. Ephrem the Syrian with prostrations to the morning and evening rule. Incense, if you use it, is used more sparingly. The Jesus Prayer becomes more central. Read the Great Canon of St. Andrew of Crete in the first week. The corner's atmosphere becomes more sober and penitential, matching the Church's Lenten fast.
♦
Holy Week
Attend the services of Holy Week at your parish if at all possible. These services cannot be replaced by home prayer — they are the heart of the Orthodox year and the fullest expression of what the prayer corner exists to sustain. Holy Week belongs to the parish; home prayer supports the heart that goes to and from those services.
★
Pascha and Bright Week
Replace the usual morning and evening prayers with the Paschal Hours throughout Bright Week. "Christ is Risen" replaces all penitential prayers. Prostrations are completely forbidden during Bright Week — the Church stands before the Risen Christ. Light more candles, use incense freely, and let joy fill the corner and the home.
○
Great Feasts
On the Twelve Great Feasts, light more candles, use incense, and read or chant the troparion and kontakion of the feast in addition to the regular rule. Let the feast enter the home — the icon of the feast, if you have it, can be placed in the center of the corner for the duration of the celebration.

Ask Your Priest

Everything in this guide is a beginning. The prayer corner that will truly form you is one established under the guidance of a priest who knows your household, your circumstances, and the state of your soul. Every family is different. A home with young children, a home where one spouse is not Orthodox, a person living alone, a person who travels constantly — each of these situations calls for a prayer rule that is appropriate and sustainable.

Your priest can help you determine which icons are appropriate to begin with, how to establish a prayer rule suited to your household, and how to adjust the rule through the seasons of the year and the seasons of your life. Home prayer is meant to support your parish life and your sacramental life — not to exist alongside them in isolation.
