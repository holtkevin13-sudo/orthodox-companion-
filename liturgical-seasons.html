Liturgical Seasons

The Orthodox year is a sacred spiral — fasting and feasting, mourning and joy, each season with its own character, fasting rule, and theological focus. Select any season to learn more.

In the Orthodox Church, time is not a line moving toward a secular horizon — it is a sacred spiral, returning each year to Pascha and, through Pascha, to the life of the age to come. The feasts anchor the seasons: the Nativity Fast exists because the Nativity of Christ exists; Great Lent exists because Pascha exists. The seasons are the Church's approach to the feasts, not the other way around.

The Church does not merely remember the events of salvation — she enters into them. At each feast, what is commemorated becomes present. Fasting, prayer, and worship are not three separate disciplines but one integrated movement of the whole person toward God. Select any season below to learn more.

Vestment color in the Orthodox Church is liturgical practice, not doctrine. The colors below reflect Greek Orthodox Archdiocese (GOA) usage, which is the reference practice for this site. Antiochian, OCA, ROCOR, Serbian, and Romanian parishes diverge in specific cases — most visibly in the days of Holy Week, the use of black, and the colors worn during the four fasts. The GOA itself observes that no formal rubric governs vestment color across the Orthodox world; common practice has shaped the scheme named here, and your own parish is the local authority.

Vestment Colors
Gold The default of the year — joy, glory, and the divine light; worn on Sundays in ordinary time and on most feasts of the Lord.
White The color of Pascha, of light, and of the Lord's burial cloth — worn at Theophany, the Transfiguration, Pascha, and at funerals, because the funeral is a passing into life.
Red The color of the martyrs' blood and of royal joy — worn on the feasts of the martyrs and, in some uses, for Pascha itself.
Purple The penitential color — worn through the weekdays of Great Lent. The bright color of Sunday returns each week, because every Sunday is a little Pascha.
Green The color of life and of the Spirit — worn on Pentecost, Palm Sunday, and the feasts of the Cross in Greek and American practice.
Blue The color of the Theotokos — worn on her feasts, after the iconographic tradition of the Mother of God in blue.

Through this rhythm, the faithful do not simply mark time, but are gradually conformed to the life of Christ.
