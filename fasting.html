Fasting Guide

The Church fasts together — through the four great seasons, every Wednesday and Friday, and on appointed days throughout the year. A guide to the Orthodox fast: its practice, its theology, and its life.

On the Orthodox Fast
Fasting is not a technique for self-improvement. It is how the Church, as one body, turns toward God — quieting the passions, sharpening prayer, and preparing the whole person through repentance toward the salvation He won for us and the joy of the Feast.

Above all, the fast prepares us to receive Christ Himself in the Holy Mysteries. Through repentance, purification, and the discipline of the body, the Church readies her children to approach the Eucharistic Cup — to receive the Body and Blood of the Lord with clean hands and a pure heart. The fast and the Eucharist are inseparable: we empty ourselves that He might fill us.
The Lord’s Teaching on Fasting
“When you fast, do not be like the hypocrites, with a sad countenance. For they disfigure their faces that they may appear to men to be fasting. But you, when you fast, anoint your head and wash your face, so that you do not appear to men to be fasting, but to your Father who is in the secret place; and your Father who sees in secret will reward you openly.”
— Matthew 6:16–18
St. Basil the Great · On Fasting
“Fasting was ordained in Paradise. The first commandment was delivered to Adam concerning fasting. Because we did not fast, we fell — let us fast, therefore, that we may return.”
— St. Basil the Great
The Four Fasting Seasons
The Weekly Fasting Rhythm

Wednesday commemorates the betrayal of Christ into the hands of sinners. Friday commemorates His Crucifixion. These are not merely dietary customs — they are the Church’s weekly participation in the Passion. To fast on Wednesday and Friday is to keep watch at Gethsemane, to stand at Golgotha. Every week the Church dies and rises with her Lord.

To fast on these days is more than not eating. It is a posture of the whole person — a turning of the heart toward repentance and prayer.

The Church does not fast on Sunday. Sunday is the Day of Resurrection — to fast on Sunday is to refuse to celebrate the victory of Christ. Every Sunday is a small Pascha.

Even on days without a formal fast, the Church calls her children to sobriety of body and soul — moderation in food, watchfulness in prayer, and a constant remembrance of God.

Days of Fasting and Abstinence
  • January 5 · Eve of TheophanyWe fast on the eve of Christ’s Baptism, preparing to receive the grace of the feast — the revelation of the Holy Trinity at the Jordan, where He sanctified the waters and renewed our nature.
  • August 29 · Beheading of St. John the BaptistWe fast in mourning for the Forerunner, whose whole life was a fast of repentance. His death by the sword bears witness to the cost of proclaiming the truth and pointing to the Lamb of God.
  • September 14 · Elevation of the Holy CrossAs the Cross is raised before the whole Church and we bow to the ground, the fast unites our bodies to the Church’s prostration — adoring the tree of life on which Christ conquered death and opened Paradise.
Seasons Without Fasting
  • Bright Weekthe week following Pascha
  • December 25 – January 4from the Nativity to the Eve of Theophany
  • Week of the Publican and Phariseea reminder that fasting without humility profits nothing
During these seasons all fasting — including the Wednesday and Friday fast — is set aside by the Church.
The Whole Fast

Fasting from food is only one part of what the Church means by fasting. The Fathers are clear: to abstain from meat while feeding on anger, gossip, or pride is not a fast — it is a diet. True fasting involves the whole person.

Prayer
The fast sharpens prayer. The body, quieted by abstinence, no longer pulls the attention downward. The Church’s fasting seasons are seasons of intensified prayer — more prostrations, more services, more silence.
Repentance
Fasting opens the heart to see itself clearly. It is preparation for confession — for the honest examination of the soul before God. Without repentance, fasting becomes spiritual theatre.
Almsgiving
What we save by fasting, we give away. The Fathers teach this consistently: fasting and almsgiving are inseparable. To fast and hoard is a contradiction. To fast and give is to share in the mercy of God.
Forgiveness
The Church begins Great Lent with Forgiveness Vespers — every Orthodox Christian asking forgiveness of every other, face to face. No fast is received by God from a heart that holds unforgiveness.
Watchfulness
Fasting is an exercise in nepsis — attentiveness to the movements of the soul. The passions quieted by hunger become visible. The fast reveals what we actually are, and gives us the tools to struggle.
What Is Permitted

These guidelines reflect the common practice of the Orthodox Church. Specific allowances vary by feast day, jurisdiction, and the guidance of your priest. The rules exist to serve the fast — not to become a burden.

The Theology of Fasting
“The Church, by a long training of fasting and instruction, prepares you for the sacred mysteries.”
— St. Cyril of Jerusalem, Catechetical Lectures
“When the stomach is contracted and does not desire much food, the mind becomes sharp and light for prayer. When the stomach is weighed down with food, thoughts become heavy and the mind becomes dull.”
— St. John of the Ladder
From the Lenten Triodion · Clean Monday Vespers
“Let us observe a fast acceptable and pleasing to the Lord. True fasting is to put away all evil, to control the tongue, to forbear from anger, to abstain from lusts, slander, falsehood, and perjury. When we abstain from these things, then is our fasting true and acceptable to God.”
A Pastoral Note

The Orthodox fast is not a law to be enforced but a medicine to be received. The Church adapts her fasting rule with compassion for those with illness, pregnancy, young children, demanding physical labor, or other difficult circumstances of life. No one should fast in a way that damages their health or becomes a source of pride.

Fasting without a spiritual father is fasting without a compass. Consult your priest about your personal fasting rule — he will help you find the measure appropriate to your life, your health, and your spiritual maturity.

Guard against three dangers the Fathers always name together: pride in your fasting, comparison with others, and legalism — the reduction of a living spiritual discipline to a checklist. The Lord’s words are the measure: “Do not be like the hypocrites.” The fast is between you and God.

A Prayer for the Fast
The Prayer of St. Ephraim the Syrian
O Lord and Master of my life,
take from me the spirit of sloth, despair, lust of power, and idle talk.

But give rather the spirit of chastity, humility, patience, and love to Thy servant.

Yea, O Lord and King, grant me to see my own transgressions,
and not to judge my brother,
for blessed art Thou, unto ages of ages. Amen.
Prayed with prostrations throughout Great Lent
