The Fasting Guide

Fasting is the oldest spiritual discipline of the Church — not a burden, but a gift. Through the fasting of the body, we awaken the hunger of the soul and open ourselves to the grace of God.

The Theology of Fasting

Orthodox fasting is not a diet. It is an act of worship — a bodily prayer offered to God in imitation of Christ, who Himself fasted forty days in the wilderness. The Church has always understood the body and soul as one united person, and fasting is the training of the whole person in self-mastery and dependence on God.

The purpose of fasting is threefold: to subdue the passions of the body, to express repentance before God, and to free the mind and heart for prayer. The Fathers consistently teach that fasting without prayer and almsgiving is an empty exercise — the three must go together.

"Fasting is the food of prayer, the nourishment of the soul, the teacher of temperance. It is the companion of watchfulness, the enemy of the passions, the intercessor for sins, the armour of the saints."
— St. John Chrysostom

Orthodox fasting traditionally means abstaining from meat, fish, dairy, wine, and oil. The degree of strictness varies by the day and season, and the Church has always been understanding of those whose health requires modification.

Fasting Rules at a Glance

◆ Wednesday & Friday Rule
Every Wednesday and Friday throughout the year is a fasting day (Strict Fast), commemorating the betrayal and crucifixion of our Lord. Exceptions apply on great feast days, the week after Pascha, and other designated fast-free periods.
◆ Great Lent (Mon–Fri)
The most strict fasting of the year. Weekdays: Strict Fast. Saturdays & Sundays: Wine and Oil. The first week and Holy Week are especially strict. Presanctified Liturgy on Wednesday and Friday evenings.
◆ Fish, Wine & Oil Allowances
Fish is allowed on: Annunciation (Mar 25), Palm Sunday, and certain feast days that fall during fasting seasons. Wine and oil are allowed on Saturdays and Sundays during most fasting seasons (except Holy Week).
◆ Strict Fast Days
Beyond the four seasons, certain individual days require strict fasting: Beheading of John the Baptist (Aug 29), Exaltation of the Holy Cross (Sep 14), and the Eve of Theophany (Jan 5) — even if they fall on Saturday or Sunday.

The Four Fasting Seasons

The Orthodox Church observes four extended fasting seasons throughout the year.

Great Lent
Clean Monday → Holy Saturday  •  48 Days
The great fast before Pascha. The strictest and most ancient of the four seasons. Abstinence from meat, fish, dairy, wine, and oil on weekdays. Sundays are more relaxed — wine and oil permitted. Holy Week is the most strict of the year.
Apostles Fast
All Saints Monday → June 28  •  Variable Length
Begins the Monday after All Saints Sunday (first Sunday after Pentecost) and ends on the eve of the feast of Sts. Peter and Paul (June 28). Length varies from 8 to 42 days depending on when Pascha falls. Moderate fast — fish, wine, and oil permitted on most days.
Dormition Fast
August 1–14  •  14 Days
The fast before the Dormition of the Theotokos (August 15). A strict but short fast — similar in character to Great Lent. Fish is permitted only on the Transfiguration (August 6). Observed with particular devotion by Greek Orthodox faithful.
Nativity Fast
November 15 → December 24  •  40 Days
The forty-day fast before the Nativity of Christ. Less strict than Great Lent — fish, wine, and oil are permitted on most days until December 20, after which the fast intensifies. The final week (Dec 20–24) mirrors the strictness of Holy Week.

Jurisdiction Variations

Note: Fasting practices vary between Orthodox jurisdictions. The Greek Orthodox (Ecumenical Patriarchate) observes the rules presented here. The OCA (Orthodox Church in America), Antiochian, Serbian, and Russian traditions follow similar principles but may differ in specific allowances — particularly regarding fish on certain days and the strictness of the Apostles Fast. Always follow the guidance of your own priest and bishop. The rules on this page reflect Greek Orthodox (New Calendar) practice.
