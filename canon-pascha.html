Canon of Holy Pascha

The Paschal Canon of St. John of Damascus — sung at the Paschal Midnight Service and used in place of morning and evening prayers throughout Bright Week.

← Back to Prayer Corner
Paschal Canon
Sung at the Paschal Midnight Service. Replaces all morning and evening prayers throughout Bright Week (the week following Pascha). Prayed at home during Paschaltide.
The Paschal Canon was composed by St. John of Damascus (676–749), the great theologian and hymnographer. It is the most joyful canon in the Orthodox tradition — nine odes of Resurrection praise, each opening with the cry "It is the Day of Resurrection!" Throughout Bright Week, this canon completely replaces the standard morning and evening prayer rule. Prostrations are forbidden during Bright Week; the faithful stand in joy.
Before the Canon
Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death, and upon those in the tombs bestowing life. (3x)
Ode 1 — Irmos
It is the day of Resurrection! Let us be illumined, O people! Pascha, the Pascha of the Lord! For from death to life, and from earth to Heaven, Christ our God has led us, as we sing the song of victory.
Ode 1 — Troparion
Let us purify our senses, and we shall behold Christ, radiant with the unapproachable light of the Resurrection, and we shall clearly hear Him say "Rejoice!" as we sing the song of victory.
Ode 3 — Irmos
Come, let us drink a new drink, not one miraculously brought forth from a barren rock, but the Fountain of incorruption, springing up from the tomb of Christ, in Whom we are established.
Ode 3 — Troparion
Now all things are filled with light: Heaven and earth and the places under the earth. Let all creation celebrate the Resurrection of Christ, in Whom it is established.
Ode 4 — Irmos
Let the divinely inspired Habakkuk stand with us on the divine watch, and show forth the radiant angel who proclaimed with a mighty voice: Today salvation has come to the world, for Christ is risen as almighty.
Ode 5 — Irmos
Let us rise in the early morning, in the early morning, and instead of myrrh offer praise to the Master, and we shall see Christ, the Sun of Righteousness, who causes life to dawn for all.
Ode 6 — Irmos
Thou didst descend into the lowest parts of the earth, and didst shatter the eternal bars that held the fettered, O Christ, and on the third day, like Jonah from the whale, Thou didst rise from the tomb.
Ode 6 — Kontakion
Even though Thou didst descend into the grave, O Immortal One, yet didst Thou destroy the power of hades; and didst rise as victor, O Christ God, calling to the myrrh-bearing women: Rejoice! and giving peace to Thine apostles, O Thou Who dost grant resurrection to the fallen.
Ode 7 — Irmos
He who delivered the Children from the furnace, having become man, suffers as a mortal, and through His suffering clothes the mortal with the beauty of incorruption: the only blessed and most glorious God of our Fathers.
Ode 8 — Irmos
This chosen and holy day is the first of the Sabbaths, the queen and lady, the feast of feasts, and the festival of festivals, in which we bless Christ forevermore.
Ode 9 — Irmos
Shine, shine, O new Jerusalem, for the glory of the Lord has risen upon thee! Dance now and be glad, O Zion, and you, pure Theotokos, delight in the resurrection of your Son.
Ode 9 — Exapostilarion
O divinely-wise women disciples, following in the wake of the myrrh-bearers, come let us glorify the life-giving Word, Who was raised from the dead, and Who with His great goodness restored life to the fallen human race.
Closing of the Paschal Canon
Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death, and upon those in the tombs bestowing life. Glory to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto the ages of ages. Amen.
