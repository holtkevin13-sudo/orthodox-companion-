The Great Canon of St. Andrew of Crete

The Great Canon of St. Andrew of Crete — the longest canon in Orthodox liturgy, a sustained meditation on repentance, sin, and mercy.

← Back to Prayer Corner
Penitential Canon
Chanted in four parts during the first week of Great Lent (Clean Monday through Thursday) and in full on the Thursday of the Fifth Week of Great Lent.
The Great Canon of St. Andrew of Crete (660–740) is the longest canon in the Orthodox liturgical tradition — 250 troparia spread across nine odes and four canticles. It is a sustained and deeply personal meditation on sin, repentance, and the mercy of God, drawing on the entire narrative of Scripture from Adam to Christ. St. Andrew addresses himself throughout in the first person, and the faithful pray with him as though the words are their own. It is one of the great masterpieces of Christian literature.
Opening Prayer
Have mercy on me, O God, have mercy on me. The Great Canon is read over four evenings during the first week of Great Lent, after Great Compline. The text below contains representative troparia from each ode; the complete canon is found in the Lenten Triodion.
Ode 1 — Irmos
He is my helper and protector, and has become my salvation. This is my God and I will glorify Him; my father's God and I will exalt Him.
Ode 1 — Selected Troparia
Where shall I begin to weep over the deeds of my wretched life? What first-fruit shall I offer, O Christ, for my present lamentation? But in Thy compassion grant me forgiveness of my sins. Come, wretched soul, with your flesh, and confess to the Creator of all. Refrain from your former brutishness and offer to God tears of repentance. I have sinned above all men. I alone have sinned against Thee. But take pity on Thy creature, O Lord, as Thou art Master and Lover of mankind. Have mercy on me, O God, have mercy on me.
Ode 2 — Irmos
Attend, O heaven, and I will speak, and I will sing of Christ Who came to dwell among us in flesh taken from a Virgin.
Ode 2 — Selected Troparia
I have darkened the beauty of my soul with the pleasures of passions, and I have made my whole mind entirely dust and ashes. I have torn the robe that the Creator wove for me in the beginning, and now I lie naked. I have clothed myself in the torn garment that the serpent wove for me by his counsel, and I am ashamed. Have mercy on me, O God, have mercy on me.
Ode 3 — Irmos
Set up in me, O Lord, the firm and unshakeable foundation of love for Thee, for Thou alone art holy and the Lord.
Ode 3 — On Adam and the Fall
Adam was deservedly expelled from Eden for transgressing one commandment of Thine. What then shall I suffer, for I am always rejecting Thy life-giving words? Have mercy on me, O God, have mercy on me.
Ode 4 — On the Flood and Noah
Of Noah hear thou, my soul: how he was well-pleasing to God and as the ruler of the world was saved, bearing in the Ark the figure of Christ, Who overthroweth corruption. Have mercy on me, O God, have mercy on me.
Ode 5 — On Moses and Israel
The lawgiver Moses smote the Egyptians with afflictions as he prayed in the desert; he drew water from the rock. Yet I have transgressed the law and through my passions have perished. Have mercy on me, O God, have mercy on me.
Ode 6 — Irmos and Kontakion
I pour out my prayer to the Lord, and to Him do I announce my grief; for my soul hath been filled with sorrow, and my life hath drawn near to hell, and I pray like Jonah: Lead me up from corruption, O God. Kontakion: My soul, my soul, arise! Why art thou sleeping? The end is drawing near, and thou wilt be confounded. Awake then and be watchful, that Christ thy God may spare thee, Who is everywhere present and filleth all things.
Ode 7 — On David, Solomon, and the Prophets
I have sinned, O Lord, I have sinned against Thee; be merciful to me. For there is no sin of man, no deed, no word, no thought, which I have not committed. Have mercy on me, O God, have mercy on me.
Ode 8 — On the New Testament
The publican cried out and was justified; and the Pharisee was condemned. The harlot obtained forgiveness; and the disciples interceded. The thief gained the Kingdom; and the dead were raised. Take me as an example for repentance, O Savior. Do not cast me away as useless. Take pity on me as Thou art compassionate, and save me. Have mercy on me, O God, have mercy on me.
Ode 9 — To the Theotokos
O most pure one, intercede with the compassionate God, that He may grant my soul forgiveness of my offenses; for thou art the Theotokos, the only hope of the faithful. Have mercy on me, O God, have mercy on me.
The Closing Troparion of the Great Canon
O Christ God, Who wast crucified of Thy own will and Who dost know the depths of each man's heart: grant me, a sinner, time to repent. Do not condemn me with those on the left at Thy dreadful Judgment. Amen.
Note
The Great Canon is chanted over four evenings during Clean Week (Monday through Thursday at Great Compline) and then in its entirety on the Thursday of the Fifth Week of Great Lent at Matins, together with the Life of St. Mary of Egypt. The complete text is found in the Lenten Triodion (available from St. Tikhon's Monastery Press).
